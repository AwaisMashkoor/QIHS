ASPIRE ¬∑ CHSC ¬∑ Term 2, 2025/26

Two Ways of Thinking About Ability

Psychologist Carol Dweck's research shows that our beliefs about ability shape how we learn and develop.

üîí Fixed Mindset

  • "I'm just not good at academic writing"
  • "Some people are natural carers ‚Äî I'm not"
  • Avoids challenges to protect self-image
  • Sees feedback as criticism
  • Gives up when things get difficult

üå± Growth Mindset

  • "I can improve my writing with practice"
  • "Caring skills develop through experience"
  • Embraces challenges as learning opportunities
  • Sees feedback as useful information
  • Persists and tries new strategies

This isn't about being positive all the time. It's about recognising that skills develop through effort, strategy, and support.

Why This Matters in Health & Social Care

üè• On Placement

You'll encounter situations you haven't faced before. A growth mindset helps you see these as learning opportunities rather than threats.

üìã Professional Development

The SSSC, NMC, and HCPC all require you to demonstrate ongoing development ‚Äî that is a growth mindset in action.

ü§ù Working with People

Believing people can change and develop is at the heart of person-centred care. It starts with believing it about yourself.

Think about it: Every experienced nurse, social worker, or paramedic was once a first-year student learning the basics. They developed their skills through practice, reflection, and support.

Setting SMART Goals

A framework for goals that actually work

ASPIRE ¬∑ CHSC ¬∑ Term 2, 2025/26

The SMART Framework

Letter Stands For Ask Yourself
S Specific What exactly do I want to achieve? Why does it matter?
M Measurable How will I know I've made progress or succeeded?
A Achievable Is this realistic for me right now, given my resources?
R Relevant Does this align with my values, programme, and career goals?
T Time-bound When will I do this by? What's my deadline?

SMART doesn't mean your goals have to be small. It means they need to be clear enough to act on.

ASPIRE ¬∑ CHSC ¬∑ Term 2, 2025/26

SMART in Practice

‚ùå Not SMART

"I want to get better at communication."

  • Not specific ‚Äî what kind of communication?
  • No way to measure progress
  • No timeline
  • Vague ‚Äî where do you start?

‚úÖ SMART

"By the end of Term 2, I will build my confidence in presenting by volunteering to present at least once during the group project and asking peers for feedback afterwards."

  • ‚úì Specific: presenting, feedback
  • ‚úì Measurable: at least once
  • ‚úì Achievable: one presentation
  • ‚úì Relevant: group project
  • ‚úì Time-bound: end of Term 2

Writing Tips for Your Action Plan

‚úÖ Do

  • Write in first person ‚Äî "I" is expected
  • Use your Week 1 self-assessment as a scaffold ‚Äî expand on what you wrote
  • Be specific about your experiences and goals
  • Use a reflection model (Gibbs, Driscoll) to structure your thinking
  • Connect your values and goals to health and social care
  • Reference sources where appropriate (Cite Them Right Harvard)

‚ùå Avoid

  • Writing generically ‚Äî this should be about you
  • Simply listing things without reflection
  • Being vague ‚Äî "I want to improve" isn't enough
  • Copying content from other students or AI tools
  • Leaving it until the last minute
  • Trying to sound perfect ‚Äî vulnerability is a strength in reflective writing

Using a Reflection Model

You were introduced to these in Week 1. Here's a quick reminder of how they help structure your Action Plan:

Driscoll's What? Model

  • What? ‚Äî Describe the situation or experience
  • So what? ‚Äî Why does it matter? What did you learn?
  • Now what? ‚Äî What will you do next?

Simple and effective ‚Äî great for goal-setting sections.

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle

  • Description ‚Üí Feelings ‚Üí Evaluation
  • ‚Üí Analysis ‚Üí Conclusion ‚Üí Action Plan

More detailed ‚Äî useful for deeper reflection on values and experiences.

Kolb's Learning Cycle

  • Concrete Experience ‚Üí Reflective Observation
  • ‚Üí Abstract Conceptualisation ‚Üí Active Experimentation

Good for linking experience to theory and planning next steps.

You don't have to use one model for the entire plan ‚Äî you can use different models for different sections.

Common Questions

"Do I need references?"

Yes, some references are expected. You might reference reflection models, professional codes (SSSC/NMC/HCPC), or academic sources about goal-setting or personal development. Quality over quantity.

"Can I change my goals later?"

Absolutely. Your goals can evolve between the formative draft and the final submission. In fact, your final reflection should discuss how and why they changed.

"What if I don't know my career path yet?"

That's completely normal. One of your classmates wrote: "As of right now I am still unsure of what type of professional I would like to become." That's honest ‚Äî and honesty is what we're looking for. Your Action Plan can include a goal about exploring career options.

"Is 1000 words a minimum or maximum?"

It's a guide. Aim for around 1000 words (¬±10%). Going significantly over suggests you need to be more concise; significantly under may mean you haven't reflected deeply enough.
