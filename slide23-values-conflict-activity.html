Margaret's Independence
Margaret, 82, with mild dementia and recent falls, insists on living alone in her two-story house. She values her independence and refuses to move or accept home care. Her daughter is extremely worried about her safety, especially using stairs. The care team is divided on whether to respect Margaret's wishes or prioritize her safety.
Patient Autonomy Values
  • Right to make own choices
  • Independence & dignity
  • Quality of life over safety
  • Home has emotional value
VS
Professional Duty of Care
  • Prevent harm & injury
  • Clinical risk assessment
  • Professional accountability
  • Family concerns valid

David's Diagnosis
David, 67, has been diagnosed with terminal cancer (6 months prognosis). His wife and adult children beg the team not to tell him the full truth, saying "it will destroy his hope." They want him to believe treatment is curative. David hasn't directly asked about prognosis but mentions planning a world cruise next year with his retirement savings.
Truth & Informed Consent
  • Right to know diagnosis
  • Informed decision-making
  • Prepare for end of life
  • Professional honesty
VS
Beneficence & Hope
  • Protect emotional wellbeing
  • Family knows him best
  • Hope aids healing
  • Cultural considerations

Priya's Care Preferences
Priya, 35, with severe learning disabilities, loves swimming and eating sweets. Her parents, who are her primary carers, have stopped both due to repeated ear infections from swimming and concerns about her weight gain. Priya becomes distressed when denied these activities. Staff feel she should have these choices, but parents insist they know what's best for their daughter.
Individual Choice & Rights
  • Personal preferences matter
  • Right to enjoyment
  • Least restrictive option
  • Person-centred approach
VS
Family Care & Protection
  • Parents as advocates
  • Long-term health concerns
  • Daily care responsibility
  • Best interests decision
