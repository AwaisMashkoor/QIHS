Part 1: Understanding Values

What guides your decisions and actions?

What Are Values?

"Values are principles and beliefs that guide our decisions and behaviour"

Values are:

  • What matters most to YOU
  • The "why" behind your choices
  • Your internal compass
  • Personally meaningful

Values are NOT:

  • Goals or outcomes
  • Rules imposed by others
  • Fixed or unchanging
  • Right or wrong

Why Values Matter in Health & Social Care

Part 3: Equality, Diversity & Inclusion

Fundamental principles for Health & Social Care

What Do We Mean by Equality, Diversity & Inclusion?

‚öñÔ∏è

Equality

Same opportunities and treatment

Everyone has equal access to services and is treated fairly

üåà

Diversity

Recognizing differences

Valuing the variety of backgrounds, experiences, and identities people bring

ü§ó

Inclusion

Everyone feels welcome

Creating environments where everyone can participate and belong

Inclusive practice is not a static state, but a way of working, being and relating to others. It is an ongoing, reflective process that is constantly developing and transforming.

Equality ‚â† Equity ‚â† Justice

Equality

"Everyone gets the same"

The assumption is that everyone benefits from the same supports.

Example: Everyone gets the same walking frame, regardless of their mobility needs

Equity

"Everyone gets what they need"

Recognizes that people have different circumstances and allocates resources accordingly.

Example: Some get wheelchairs, some get crutches, based on their needs

Justice

"Fix the system"

All can access what they need because the causes of inequity have been addressed.

Example: Buildings are accessible, transport is available, societal barriers removed

In H&SC, we aim for EQUITY and work toward JUSTICE

Why Does EDI Matter in Health & Social Care?

  • Legal Requirement: Equality Act 2010 protects people from discrimination
  • Professional Duty: SSSC Codes of Practice require anti-discriminatory practice
  • Better Care: Understanding diverse needs leads to better outcomes
  • Service User Rights: Everyone deserves dignity, respect, and quality care
  • Reducing Inequalities: Care professionals can challenge unfair systems
H&SC Example

An older adult from a minoritized ethnic background may face:

  • Language barriers accessing services
  • Cultural preferences not being understood
  • Discrimination based on age AND ethnicity (intersectionality)

An inclusive care worker: Arranges interpreter services, learns about cultural practices, advocates for the person's needs

Part 4: Health Inequalities in Society

Understanding unfair differences in health

What Are Health Inequalities?

Definition

Health inequalities are unfair and avoidable differences in health across the population, and between different groups within society.

Examples of Health Inequalities

  • Life expectancy varies by 10+ years between richest and poorest areas
  • Higher rates of mental illness in deprived communities
  • Disabled people face barriers accessing healthcare
  • Some ethnic groups experience worse health outcomes

Key Point

These differences are NOT due to biology or individual choice alone. They result from unfair social, economic, and environmental conditions.

Social Determinants of Health

The conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age that affect their health

üí∞

Income & Employment

  • Poverty limits access to healthy food, warm housing
  • Job insecurity increases stress
üè†

Housing & Environment

  • Damp, overcrowded housing affects health
  • Pollution in deprived areas
üìö

Education

  • Higher education = better health literacy
  • Educational inequality perpetuates cycles
ü©∫

Healthcare Access

  • Transport barriers
  • Digital exclusion
  • Discrimination
üë•

Social Networks

  • Isolation affects mental health
  • Community support protects health
‚öñÔ∏è

Discrimination

  • Racism impacts health
  • Stigma prevents help-seeking

Health Inequalities in Scotland: The Evidence

Life Expectancy Gap

A baby boy born in the most deprived areas of Scotland can expect to live 13 years less than one born in the least deprived areas.

Drug-Related Deaths

Scotland has the highest rate in Europe. Deaths are concentrated in deprived communities.

Mental Health

Rates of depression and anxiety are twice as high in deprived areas compared to affluent areas.

Child Poverty

24% of children in Scotland live in poverty, affecting their development and future health.

COVID-19 Impact

Death rates in the most deprived areas were more than double those in the least deprived.

Why This Matters for YOUR Practice

üîç

Person-Centred Approach

Advocacy

Practice Example

Scenario: A patient keeps missing diabetes appointments.
