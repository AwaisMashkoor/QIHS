Foundations

What Makes an Interview?

An interview is not simply a conversation. It is a purposeful, planned interaction with identifiable goals, structure, and professional accountability.

Purpose Clarity

Both parties must understand why the interaction is taking place. In healthcare, unclear purpose leads to missed information and patient anxiety.

Preparation

Structure, content, and conduct must be planned in advance. Hargie (2019) argues that preparation is the single strongest predictor of interview effectiveness.

Documentation

Information gathered must be accurately recorded. In NHS Scotland, this is both a professional and legal requirement under NMC Code Sections 10 and 25.

Critical question: If an interview is purposeful and planned, what happens when time pressures in a busy NHS ward force nurses to conduct interviews without preparation? Is the interaction still an 'interview' — or has it become something else?

Hargie, O. (2019) Skilled Interpersonal Communication, 7th edn. London: Routledge. | NMC (2018) The Code, Sections 2, 7, 10, 25.

Foundations

Interview vs Conversation: Why the Distinction Matters

When you chat with a friend about their weekend, is that an interview? When you ask a patient about their pain at the bedside, is that a conversation?

Dimension Conversation Interview
Purpose Social connection, enjoyment, information exchange Specific, predetermined goal
Structure Spontaneous, free-flowing Planned stages (opening → body → closing)
Roles Equal, interchangeable Distinct: interviewer guides, interviewee responds
Recording Not expected Essential — professional and legal requirement
Accountability None — no professional obligation Interviewer is accountable for outcomes and documentation
The danger zone: Informal bedside chats where clinically significant information is disclosed but not recorded — it looked like a conversation, but it was an interview you didn't recognise.
The skill: Making interviews feel conversational while maintaining professional purpose and structure. The patient shouldn't feel interrogated — but you must remain accountable for the information.

Critical point: These categories are not mutually exclusive. A diagnostic interview may uncover adherence issues requiring motivational interviewing. Recognising when the interview purpose shifts is a hallmark of skilled practice.
Types & Purposes

Information-Seeking Interviews

Diagnostic

Focuses on current health problems using systematic questioning. Includes symptom exploration, comprehensive history (personal and family), physical examination, and accurate record-keeping. Clinical example: A&E triage assessment for chest pain.

Health Appraisal

Broader than diagnostic — examines physical, psychological, social, economic, spiritual, and environmental factors. Relies heavily on open questions. Clinical example: Comprehensive nursing admission assessment.

Induction

Orientates new staff to the organisation, identifies learning gaps, and establishes positive working relationships. Your experience: Think about your own NHS placement inductions.

Performance Appraisal

Reviews past performance and sets future goals. In NHS Scotland, linked to the Knowledge and Skills Framework (KSF). Increasingly part of qualified nurses' mentoring responsibilities.

Research

Collects data to answer research questions. Requires careful attention to ethics, informed consent, and the interviewer's dual role as researcher and practitioner.

Reflect: Which of these information-seeking interviews have you experienced — as interviewer or interviewee? What made the difference between an effective and ineffective experience?
Types & Purposes

Problem-Solving Interviews

These interviews go beyond gathering information — the interviewer is actively working towards resolution, change, or decision-making.

Counselling Interview

Explores problems, clarifies issues, and supports the client toward action. Usually involves a series of interviews. This is the interview form that underpins Egan's Skilled Helper and Rogers' Person-Centred Therapy — both covered in Weeks 7 and 9.

Disciplinary Interview

Addresses unacceptable behaviour through a structured process. Stressful for both parties. Requires particular sensitivity to power dynamics and procedural fairness.

Selection Interview

Matches candidates to roles. The consequences of poor interviewing here are significant — selecting the wrong person can harm team effectiveness and patient safety.

Motivational Interview

A specialised form of counselling interview for addictive or harmful behaviours. Aims to evoke the client's own motivation for change rather than imposing it. ▶ We'll explore MI in depth in Week 10

Miller, W.R. & Rollnick, S. (2013) Motivational Interviewing, 3rd edn. New York: Guilford Press. | Egan, G. (2013) The Skilled Helper, 10th edn.

Structure

Interview Formats: A Continuum of Control

Format Purpose Characteristics Healthcare Example
Structured Obtain specific, standardised information Planned agenda; questions decided in advance; interviewer controls pace and content Triage assessment, research questionnaire, medication reconciliation
Semi-structured Gain information while promoting patient participation Flexible agenda; some questions pre-planned; pace shared between interviewer and interviewee Nursing admission, performance appraisal, care planning discussion
Unstructured Explore the client's own perception of issues General area of enquiry identified; few pre-set questions; content and pace largely determined by client Counselling sessions, initial mental health assessment, bereavement support
Evidence base: The Calgary-Cambridge model — a semi-structured approach — is considered best practice for medical interviewing because it balances systematic information-gathering with patient-centred relationship-building (Silverman, Kurtz & Draper, 2013). Many NHS Scotland clinical settings have adopted this framework.

Silverman, J., Kurtz, S. & Draper, J. (2013) Skills for Communicating with Patients, 3rd edn. London: CRC Press.

Structure

Question Types in the Interview Context

Connecting Week 4's communication skills to purposeful interviewing — the funnel technique:

Open
"Tell me about how you've been feeling"
→
Probing
"You mentioned tiredness — tell me more about that"
→
Focused
"When did the tiredness start?"
→
Closed
"Rate your energy 1–10 today?"

The Funnel Approach

Start broad, then narrow progressively. This lets the patient set the initial agenda before the interviewer focuses on clinical priorities. Evidence shows this approach yields more accurate and complete information than starting with closed questions (Silverman et al., 2013).

Questions to Avoid

Leading: "You're feeling better now, aren't you?" — directs the patient toward your expected answer.

Multiple: "How are you feeling, have you been eating, and are you sleeping?" — which one does the patient answer?

Silverman, J., Kurtz, S. & Draper, J. (2013) Skills for Communicating with Patients. | Connects to Week 4: Communication Skills.

Structure

Setting the Scene: Before the Interview Begins

The stages students most often skip are the ones that determine whether the rest will work:

Preparing Yourself

Review available records — what do you already know? What gaps need filling?

Clarify your purpose — can you state in one sentence why you're conducting this interview?

Check your assumptions — what are you expecting to find? Are you open to being surprised?

Preparing the Environment

Privacy: Can the patient speak freely? Curtains, doors, appropriate space.

Positioning: Eye level. No physical barriers. Appropriate distance — close enough to convey interest, far enough for comfort.

Timing: Is this the right moment? Is the patient in pain, anxious, or about to receive visitors?

Preparing the Patient

Set Induction (Hargie, 2019): Orient the interviewee so they know what to expect — like setting the GPS before driving.

Introduce yourself · State the purpose · Explain the process · Indicate duration · Invite questions · Seek consent to proceed

NMC Code Section 4: Act in the best interests of people — this starts before the first question.

The ward reality: What happens when there isn't time for proper preparation? Research suggests that even 60 seconds of mental preparation — clarifying purpose and checking assumptions — significantly improves interview quality (Maguire & Pitceathly, 2002).
Skills in Practice

Applied Skills: The Health Appraisal Interview

The interview most nursing students will conduct regularly on placement. Here's what each stage looks like in practice:

Opening Stage

Put patient at ease · Explain purpose and process · Identify topic areas · Encourage full participation · Record personal details

"Hello, I'm [name]. I'd like to ask about your health so we can plan the best care for you. This will take about 20 minutes. Is that alright?"

Main Body

Work through agenda · Use open questions to explore topics · Probe when necessary · Listen actively · Signal transitions between topics · Record key information

Open → focused: "Tell me about your general health" → "You mentioned breathlessness — when does that tend to happen?"

Closing Stage

Review what's been discussed · Allow patient to comment and correct · Inform about next steps and what happens with their information · Bring interaction to a close

"Let me check I've understood everything correctly... Is there anything I've missed or you'd like to add?"

Adapted from Sully, P. & Dallas, J. (2010) Essential Communication Skills for Nursing and Midwifery. Edinburgh: Elsevier.

Skills in Practice

Applied Skills: The Counselling Interview

Notice how the counselling interview differs from health appraisal — the client has much more control over pace and direction:

Opening Stage

Welcome the client · Confirm confidentiality · Clarify respective roles (you are not the expert fixing them) · Explain the form the interview will take

Notice: establishing confidentiality and role clarity is unique to counselling — you wouldn't necessarily do this in a diagnostic interview.

Main Body

Convey attention and interest · Help client identify the problem · Facilitate exploration · Seek mutual understanding · Assess readiness for solutions · Explore alternatives · Assist in selecting best options · Outline continuing support

Closing Stage

Agree actions for both parties · Summarise main points · Agree on next meeting

Note the mutuality: both parties take action. This reflects the non-directive philosophy that underpins person-centred practice.

Forward link — Egan's Skilled Helper (Week 7): This counselling interview structure maps directly onto Egan's three-stage model: Story (exploring the problem) → New Perspectives (understanding what's happening) → Action (what to do about it).

Side by Side: Two Interview Approaches

Who controls direction Mainly the interviewer — working through an agenda Mainly the client — following their concerns
Format Semi-structured (planned but flexible) Unstructured (client sets direction)

Critical Analysis

When Interviews Go Wrong: Interviewer Issues

Bias

Using leading questions to elicit desired answers. Selectively responding — verbally and non-verbally — to certain information. In healthcare, unconscious bias affects pain assessment, treatment decisions, and patient trust.

Example: Responding more enthusiastically to 'I've been taking my medication' than to 'I stopped because of side effects.'

Incompetence

Over-reliance on closed questions. Using inappropriate language or jargon. Interrupting. Research suggests clinicians interrupt patients within an average of 11 seconds of their opening statement.

Power Dynamics

The interviewer typically holds more power — through professional status, environmental control, and information asymmetry. This can inhibit the flow of information and create a 'pleasing the professional' dynamic.

Attribution Errors

Dispositional attribution: 'This patient is non-compliant' (blaming their character). Situational attribution: 'This patient may not have understood the instructions' (considering context). Skilled interviewers challenge their own attributions.

Marvel, M.K. et al. (1999) 'Soliciting the Patient's Agenda', JAMA, 281(3), pp. 283–287. | Hoffman, K.M. et al. (2016) 'Racial bias in pain assessment', PNAS, 113(16).

Critical Analysis

When Interviews Go Wrong: Interviewee Issues

Understanding why patients may not communicate accurately is as important as recognising that they don't:

Inaccurate Responses

May be unintentional (patient doesn't know the right information) or deliberate (withholding due to fear, embarrassment, or stigma). In healthcare, think about: alcohol intake disclosure, medication adherence, pain levels, domestic circumstances.

Social Desirability Bias

Patients underreport behaviours they perceive as socially unacceptable and overreport 'healthy' behaviours. The power dynamic amplifies this — patients want to be seen as 'good patients' by their healthcare professional.

Social Perception & Influence

The interviewee's perception of the interviewer's status, attitudes, and expectations shapes their responses. Patients may try to impress, please, or avoid conflict rather than communicate accurately.

The connection to Week 3: The therapeutic relationship — built on Rogers' core conditions of empathy, congruence, and unconditional positive regard — is what creates the conditions for honest, accurate patient communication. Without it, even technically skilled interviewing produces unreliable data.

Tourangeau, R. & Yan, T. (2007) 'Sensitive Questions in Surveys', Psychological Bulletin, 133(5), pp. 859–883.

Practical Strategies

Overcoming Barriers: Evidence-Informed Strategies

Knowing what can go wrong is essential — but so is knowing what to do about it:

Reducing Interviewer Bias

Use the funnel approach: Start with open questions to let the patient tell their story, then narrow to focused and closed questions only when needed. This reduces the risk of leading the patient toward your assumptions.

Monitor your non-verbals: Respond with equal interest to all answers — not just the ones that confirm your hypothesis.

Creating Psychological Safety

Normalise disclosure: "Many patients find it difficult to talk about..." reduces shame and signals that honest answers are expected and valued.

Embrace silence: Research shows patients often share their most significant information after a pause of 3–5 seconds. Resist the urge to fill every silence.

Environmental Preparation

Physical setup matters: Ensure privacy (curtains, closed doors). Sit at eye level — never stand over a patient. Remove physical barriers (desks, clipboards held like shields). Minimise interruptions.

Realistic Medicine implication: The environment signals whether you genuinely want to hear the patient's perspective.

Self-Awareness Strategies

Check your attributions: Before labelling a patient as 'difficult' or 'non-compliant', ask: "What situational factors might explain this behaviour?"

Reflect post-interview: What went well? Where did I lose the patient? What would I do differently? This is the reflective practice that will underpin your Category 2 assignment.

Maguire, P. & Pitceathly, C. (2002) 'Key communication skills and how to acquire them', BMJ, 325(7366), pp. 697–700.

Contemporary Context

Interviewing in 2026: Challenges for Practice

Digital Transformation

Telephone triage, video consultations, and digital health assessments are now routine in NHS Scotland. How do we maintain interview quality when we can't fully read non-verbal cues? How do we build rapport through a screen?

Consider: digital exclusion and health literacy as barriers to equitable care

Time & Workforce Pressures

Post-pandemic workforce challenges mean nurses conduct interviews under greater time pressure with less support. The tension between efficiency (getting through the checklist) and person-centredness (following the patient's lead) is a daily reality.

Cultural Competence

Scotland's increasingly diverse population requires interviewing across language barriers, cultural norms about health disclosure, and different expectations of the professional relationship. Skilled interviewing demands cultural humility, not just cultural awareness.

"Realistic Medicine asks us to move from 'What's the matter with you?' to 'What matters to you?' — but that shift requires time, skill, and interview structures that genuinely enable patient voice."
Adapted from Scottish Government, Realising Realistic Medicine (2022)

Scottish Government (2022) Realising Realistic Medicine. | NHS Education for Scotland (2023) Digital Health and Care Strategy.

Evidence-Based Framework

The Calgary-Cambridge Model

An evidence-based framework that integrates today's learning into a unified model. Notice the two parallel tracks — process and relationship must run simultaneously:

Process Track (The Skeleton)
1. Initiating the Session
2. Gathering Information
3. Physical Examination
4. Explanation & Planning
5. Closing the Session
Relationship Track (The Heart)

Providing structure — making organisation overt, attending to flow, signposting transitions

Building relationship — developing rapport, involving the patient, demonstrating empathy (Rogers' core conditions in action)

The key insight: Most interviews fail not because of poor process but because of poor relationship. Technical skill without relational skill produces compliant but disengaged patients.

Silverman, J., Kurtz, S. & Draper, J. (2013) Skills for Communicating with Patients, 3rd edn. Widely adopted across NHS Scotland medical and nursing education.

Synthesis

Bringing It Together

What We've Covered Today

Interviewing is purposeful, planned communication — distinct from everyday conversation. The type of interview (information-seeking vs. problem-solving), the format (structured to unstructured), and the five-stage structure all shape how effectively we communicate with patients.

The Critical Edge

Technical skill is necessary but insufficient. Effective interviewing also requires awareness of bias, power dynamics, attribution errors, cultural sensitivity, and the realities of contemporary healthcare delivery in NHS Scotland.
