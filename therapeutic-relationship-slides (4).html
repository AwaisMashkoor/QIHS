What is a therapeutic relationship and why does it matter?

Defining the Therapeutic Relationship

Also known as the therapeutic alliance, helping alliance, or working alliance.

A purposeful, supportive and healing association between a healthcare professional and a client that is interactive, holistic, and grounded in theory, research, and practice.

Purposeful

Goal-directed, not accidental

Collaborative

Both parties contribute

Boundaried

Professional, not personal

Time-limited

Has beginning, middle, end

What makes a relationship therapeutic?

Theoretical Foundations

Two Influential Frameworks

Carl Rogers (1957)

Person-Centred Therapy

Three core conditions necessary for therapeutic change:

  • Congruence ‚Äî genuineness, authenticity
  • Unconditional Positive Regard ‚Äî acceptance without judgement
  • Empathy ‚Äî understanding client's frame of reference

Edward Bordin (1979)

Working Alliance Model

Three interdependent components:

  • Goals ‚Äî agreement on aims of the work
  • Tasks ‚Äî agreement on activities/methods
  • Bond ‚Äî trust, respect, liking between parties

Critical Consideration

Rogers developed his theory for psychotherapy in 1950s America. To what extent does it translate to a busy NHS ward or time-pressured A&E encounter?

Rogers' Core Conditions

These must be both felt by the practitioner and perceived by the client to be effective.

What they sound like in practice:

Congruence (Genuineness)

Being authentic in the relationship. Your outward behaviour matches your inner experience.

"I have to be honest with you ‚Äî I'm not sure what the best answer is here. Let me find out and get back to you properly."

Unconditional Positive Regard

Accepting the person without judgement. Valuing them regardless of behaviour or circumstances.

"I can see this has been really difficult for you. Whatever's happened before, we're here to help you now."

Empathy

Understanding the client's experience from their perspective ‚Äî sensing their inner world "as if" it were your own.

"It sounds like you're feeling overwhelmed ‚Äî like everything's piling up at once and you don't know where to start."

Understanding Empathy

Empathy: More Than Feeling

"To sense the client's inner world of private personal meanings as if they were your own, but without ever losing the 'as if' quality."

‚Äî Carl Rogers

Patient says: "I just found out I need surgery. I don't know how I'm going to cope ‚Äî I've got nobody to help me at home."

‚úì Empathic Response

"That sounds really frightening ‚Äî facing surgery is hard enough, but worrying about managing afterwards on your own must make it feel even more overwhelming."

What this does: Names the emotion, acknowledges the specific concern, validates the difficulty.

‚úó Non-Empathic Responses

"Don't worry, you'll be fine ‚Äî people have this surgery all the time."

(Minimises, dismisses concern)

"Have you thought about asking a neighbour to help?"

(Jumps to problem-solving before acknowledging feeling)

Maintaining the Relationship

What keeps a therapeutic relationship effective over time?

It's often the small, consistent things that matter most.

Active Listening

Full attention, verbal and non-verbal responsiveness, checking understanding

Looks like: Putting down notes, making eye contact, "Let me check I've understood..."

Maintaining Security

Creating psychological safety where patients feel secure to share

Looks like: "There's no wrong answer" or "This stays between us unless there's a safety concern"

Being Genuine

Consistent, honest, authentic presence ‚Äî building trust through reliability

Looks like: "I said I'd find out ‚Äî here's what I learned" or "I don't know, but I'll find someone who does"

Demonstrating Care

Actions that communicate the patient matters ‚Äî remembering details, following up

Looks like: "How did your daughter's exam go?" or checking in after a difficult conversation

Part 3

Boundaries & Power

Navigating the complexities of professional relationships

Power Dynamics

The Nature of Professional Relationships

Not an Equal Relationship

Different roles characterised by different forms of power:

  • Expert power ‚Äî knowledge asymmetry
  • Legitimate power ‚Äî professional authority
  • Positional power ‚Äî gatekeeper to services
  • Physical power ‚Äî patient vulnerability

Self-Awareness is Essential

Awareness of our attitudes, prejudices, and behavioural patterns is essential for honest relationships.

Communication Reflects Relationship

Where there is mutual respect, communication is positive. Where there is mistrust, this shows in argument and reluctance to listen.

Communication Patterns

Controlling vs Facilitative Styles

Scenario: Patient hasn't been taking their blood pressure medication regularly.

Controlling:

"You need to take these tablets every day. If you don't, you'll have a stroke. I'm going to refer you to the pharmacist for a compliance review."

Facilitative:

"I notice you've missed some doses. Can you tell me what's getting in the way? Let's see if we can find an approach that works better for your life."

Controlling Style Facilitative Style
Patient should comply Seeks collaboration
Patient's input not valued Patient's input is essential
Professional as authority Professional as partner

Critical Question

Is facilitative always appropriate? Consider: emergencies, cognitive impairment, safeguarding.

Recognising Patterns

Behaviours That Undermine Relationships

1. Dominating

Stream of talk, not eliciting patient's view

"Right, so what we're going to do is... and then... and also..."

2. Blocking

Changing subject, shutting down emotion

"Anyway, let's focus on your medication..."

3. Exaggerating

Over-emphasising to force compliance

"If you don't do this, you'll definitely end up back in hospital."

4. Arguing

Opposing before understanding

"No, that's not right ‚Äî the research clearly shows..."

5. Dismissing

Minimising patient concerns

"I wouldn't worry about that if I were you."

6. Interrupting

Cutting off before they finish

"Yes, yes ‚Äî I know what you mean. So..."

Body Language

Non-verbal communication reveals what words may hide.

Reading Patients

Body language tells us more than words alone. Note: physical conditions may limit patients' ability to gesture.

Incongruence

Sometimes people want to communicate the opposite of what their body shows. A nervous professional may try to appear confident but shake or avoid eye contact.

Key Point

Patients detect incongruence between what we say and how we appear. Self-awareness of our non-verbal communication is essential.

Professional Boundaries

Therapeutic Boundaries

To remain separate, as opposed to being emotionally distant, is a key aspect of the therapeutic relationship.

Why Boundaries Matter

If patients perceive us as vulnerable or 'unsafe' in coping with emotional distress, they may not disclose their anxieties.

Patients need to feel we are robust enough to contain difficult emotions.

Stress & Sustainability

Protecting your capacity for therapeutic relationships

Practitioner Wellbeing

Burnout and the Therapeutic Relationship

Same nurse, same patient concern ‚Äî six months apart:

Before burnout:

"I can hear you're really worried about going home. Tell me more about what's concerning you ‚Äî let's work through this together."

After burnout:

"The discharge plan is all sorted. The district nurse will visit. You'll be fine."

What is Burnout?

Emotional exhaustion ‚Äî nothing left to give

Depersonalisation ‚Äî patients become "the hip in bed 4"

Reduced accomplishment ‚Äî nothing feels worthwhile

The Research

Negative correlation between burnout and empathy in healthcare professionals.

Wilkinson et al. (2017)

Critical Point

Burnout is not personal failure. It's often a system design problem.

Recognition

Stressors and Warning Signs

Common Stressors

  • Unrealistic expectations and demands
  • Confronting own mortality
  • Constantly saying goodbye
  • Patients and families not coping
  • Interprofessional conflict
  • Ethical dilemmas
  • Pressure to get it right every time

Warning Signs

  • Inability to concentrate
  • Loss of energy / chronic fatigue
  • Preoccupation with work
  • Disturbed sleep pattern
  • Irritability and mood swings
  • Inability to relax
  • Sense that life is a fight, not joy
Sustainability

Protecting Your Capacity

Collegial Support

Share with colleagues where you are with patients rather than defending what you're doing.

You either win or learn ‚Äî there is nothing to lose.

Avoid Socialisation into Dysfunction

We can become so socialised into our clinical world we no longer see its faults. Fresh perspective matters.

Individual Strategies

  • Regular supervision and reflection
  • Clear work-life boundaries
  • Physical self-care
  • Recognising warning signs early

Systemic Strategies

  • Manageable caseloads
  • Protected supervision time
  • Team debriefing
