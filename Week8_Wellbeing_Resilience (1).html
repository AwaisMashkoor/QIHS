The Care Context

Why This Matters for You

Health and social care is one of the most rewarding career paths — but it's also one of the most emotionally demanding.

The reality:

  • You'll work with people in crisis, pain, and loss
  • Emotional labour is constant and often invisible
  • Staff shortages and time pressure are real
  • Boundaries between caring and over-giving blur easily

Professional body expectations:

  • SSSC: Workers should look after their own health and wellbeing
  • NMC: Nurses must prioritise self-care to practise safely
  • HCPC: Registrants must manage their own health to maintain fitness to practise

Wellbeing isn't a luxury or a soft topic. It's a professional responsibility.

Core Care Skill

Empathy & Understanding

Empathy is the ability to understand and share the feelings of another person. In care, it's not optional — it's foundational.

Empathy vs Sympathy:

  • Sympathy: "I feel sorry for you"
  • Empathy: "I'm trying to understand what this is like for you"

Empathy connects. Sympathy creates distance.

Practising Empathy

  • Listen actively — without planning your response
  • Acknowledge feelings — "That sounds really difficult"
  • Avoid fixing — sometimes people need to be heard, not solved
  • Be curious — "Tell me more about that"
  • Check assumptions — your experience isn't everyone's

But: Empathy without boundaries leads to emotional exhaustion. That's where compassion fatigue comes in.

Understanding the Risks

Compassion Fatigue & Burnout

🔥

Burnout

Gradual exhaustion from prolonged workplace stress. Builds slowly over time.

  • Emotional exhaustion
  • Feeling detached or cynical
  • Reduced sense of achievement
  • Physical symptoms (fatigue, headaches)
  • "I just can't do this anymore"
💔

Compassion Fatigue

The emotional cost of caring for people who are suffering. Can develop more suddenly.

  • Reduced empathy
  • Emotional numbness
  • Difficulty separating work from home
  • Intrusive thoughts about clients/patients
  • "I don't care anymore" (but you do)

Important: These are normal human responses to abnormal levels of emotional demand. They are not signs of weakness. Recognising them early is a professional skill.

ASPIRE CHSC • UWS Paisley

Early Warning Signs

How Would You Know?

🧠 Emotional

  • Feeling overwhelmed or helpless
  • Irritability or mood swings
  • Loss of motivation
  • Dreading work or study

💪 Physical

  • Constant tiredness
  • Frequent illness
  • Sleep problems
  • Changes in appetite

🤝 Social

  • Withdrawing from friends/family
  • Avoiding colleagues or classmates
  • Feeling isolated
  • Loss of interest in social activities

📋 Behavioural

  • Missing deadlines or classes
  • Procrastinating more than usual
  • Increased use of alcohol or other coping mechanisms
  • Difficulty concentrating

Reflection prompt: Have you noticed any of these in yourself recently? In a friend or classmate? What would you do?

Practical Strategies

Finding a Balance

Balance doesn't mean everything gets equal time. It means being intentional about what matters and noticing when things are tipping.

The Balance Wheel

Consider how much time and energy you give to each area:

  • Study & academic work
  • Paid work / placement
  • Relationships & social life
  • Physical health
  • Rest & recovery
  • Hobbies & enjoyment

Signs You're Out of Balance

  • One area dominates everything
  • You've dropped things you used to enjoy
  • You feel guilty when you're not studying
  • You never feel "done" for the day
  • Small problems feel overwhelming

Key point: Balance isn't a destination — it's something you keep adjusting. Being aware of the imbalance is the first step.

Understanding Resilience

What Resilience Actually Means

Resilience is NOT:

  • Never feeling stressed or upset
  • Being "tough" or unemotional
  • Pushing through no matter what
  • A fixed personality trait you're born with
  • An excuse for poor working conditions

Resilience IS:

  • The ability to adapt and recover from difficulty
  • A skill that can be learned and strengthened
  • Knowing when to ask for help
  • Having strategies ready before you need them
  • Accepting setbacks as part of the process

In care settings: Resilience is recognised by SSSC, NMC and HCPC as essential for sustainable, safe practice. It protects both you and the people you work with.

ASPIRE CHSC • UWS Paisley

Practical Approaches

Building Confidence & Resilience

1

Reframe setbacks
A failed assignment isn't proof you can't do this. It's information about what to work on next.

2

Build your support network
Know who you can turn to — friends, family, lecturers, services. Don't wait for a crisis.

3

Develop a routine
Predictable structure reduces decision fatigue and creates space for recovery.

4

Celebrate small wins
Submitted on time? Attended every class this week? That counts. Acknowledge progress.

5

Set boundaries
Saying "no" to protect your energy is not selfish. It's professional self-management.

6

Practise self-compassion
Talk to yourself the way you'd talk to a friend. You deserve the same kindness you give others.

LO4 Connection

Dealing with Challenge & Change

University is a period of significant change. So is entering the care workforce. Learning to navigate uncertainty is a core professional skill.

Common Challenges You'll Face

  • Academic pressure and deadlines
  • Financial stress
  • Adjusting to new environments
  • Balancing study with work/family
  • Dealing with difficult placement experiences
  • Uncertainty about the future

Strategies for Navigating Change

  • Accept uncertainty — not everything needs to be figured out now
  • Focus on what you can control — your effort, your attitude, your next step
  • Break it down — overwhelming problems become manageable tasks
  • Talk about it — sharing reduces the weight
  • Look back at past challenges — you've survived 100% of your worst days so far
LO4: Recognise how changes shape academic and professional environments

A Professional Strength

Asking for Help

In care professions, asking for help isn't weakness — it's a core professional skill. Supervision, debriefing, and peer support are built into care practice for a reason.

Why People Don't Ask

  • "I should be able to handle this"
  • "Everyone else seems fine"
  • "I don't want to be a burden"
  • "They'll think I'm not coping"
  • "I don't know who to ask"

How to Ask

  • Be specific: "I'm struggling with X, can you help me with Y?"
  • Be honest: "I'm not coping as well as I'd like"
  • Start small: You don't have to share everything at once
  • Choose your person: Friend, lecturer, support service — pick who fits
  • Remember: The people who help others for a living understand this better than anyone

Practice now: Think of one thing you could use help with. Who would you ask?

Week 9 Preview

Group Presentation: What to Expect

📋

Format

  • 10–15 minutes per group
  • All group members must contribute
  • Format is flexible: slides, video, poster, website, etc.
  • Followed by brief Q&A / feedback
✅

What We're Looking For

  • Clear connection to your topic and person-centred care
  • Evidence of collaboration (not just divided labour)
  • Everyone's voice is represented
  • Engagement with the theme, not perfection

Remember: The presentation itself isn't assessed. But your reflection on the experience is. So pay attention to how the process feels — the challenges, the teamwork, what you're learning about yourself and others. That's the material for your Group Project Reflection (750 words).

ASPIRE CHSC • UWS Paisley
👥

Group Project Work Time

Use this time to finalise your presentation with your group.

Focus on:

  1. Content: Is your argument clear? Does it link to person-centred care?
  2. Structure: Who's presenting which section? What's the flow?
  3. Practicalities: Do you need to test tech? Print anything? Bring props?
  4. Rehearsal: Run through it once. Time yourselves. Identify weak spots.

Reflection prep: While you work, notice: How are decisions being made? Who leads? Who listens? Are there tensions? This is all material for your 750-word reflection.
