Collaboration in Health & Social Care

"No single profession has all the skills needed to meet the complex needs of the people we support."

In your career, you will work alongside:

  • Nurses, midwives, and health visitors
  • Social workers and care managers
  • Allied health professionals (OTs, physios, speech therapists)
  • Support workers and care assistants
  • GPs, consultants, and pharmacists
üè•

Why It Matters

  • Better outcomes for people who use services
  • Reduced duplication and errors
  • Holistic, person-centred care
  • Shared decision-making
  • Professional learning from each other

When It Goes Wrong

Serious case reviews consistently highlight poor communication and lack of collaborative working as key factors

What to Expect: Tuckman's Stages

Every group goes through these stages ‚Äî it's completely normal.

üå±

1. Forming

Polite, cautious, getting to know each other. People are finding their feet and avoiding conflict.

"Everyone's being really nice"

‚ö°

2. Storming

Disagreements emerge. Different ideas, working styles, and expectations clash. This is normal and necessary.

"Why can't we just agree?"

ü§ù

3. Norming

The group finds its rhythm. Roles are clear, trust builds, and people start working together effectively.

"We're getting the hang of this"

üöÄ

4. Performing

The group is productive, supportive, and focused. People know their roles and collaborate with confidence.

"We've got this!"

The key message: If your group hits a rough patch, that's storming ‚Äî it means you're progressing, not failing. Work through it, don't avoid it.

Working Effectively in Your Team

üí¨

Communication

  • Listen actively ‚Äî hear people out before responding
  • Be clear about what you need and expect
  • Check in regularly ‚Äî don't assume everyone's okay
  • Give constructive feedback ‚Äî focus on the work, not the person
‚öñÔ∏è

Decision-Making

  • Everyone gets a voice ‚Äî invite quieter members in
  • Discuss options before jumping to a decision
  • Compromise ‚Äî you won't always get your first choice
  • Commit to group decisions even if you initially disagreed
üîß

When Things Go Wrong

  • Address problems early ‚Äî don't let them build up
  • Use "I" statements ‚Äî "I feel..." not "You always..."
  • If someone's disengaging ‚Äî reach out with concern, not blame
  • Come talk to me if your group is really struggling

üè• In Health & Social Care: These are exactly the skills you'll use in multidisciplinary team meetings, handovers, case conferences, and daily practice. Developing them now prepares you for professional life.

Activity: Create Your Team Charter

A team charter is a simple agreement about how you'll work together. Discuss and agree on these points as a group:

üìã Your Team Charter

  • Team members: Names and contact details
  • Communication: How will you stay in touch?
  • Meetings: When and where will you meet?
  • Roles: Who's taking which project role? (Coordinator, Research Lead, etc.)
  • Deadlines: When will different parts be ready?
  • Ground rules: What do you expect from each other?
