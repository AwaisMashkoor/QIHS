Understanding Communication in Healthcare Context

Defining Communication: Multiple Perspectives

Communication is a contested concept with no single agreed definition. Understanding multiple perspectives helps us appreciate its complexity.

Transmission Model

"The process of sending and receiving messages through verbal and non-verbal means."

Limitation: Assumes linear, one-way flow; ignores context and interpretation.

Interactional Model

"A dynamic process where participants alternate as sender and receiver, with feedback loops."

Limitation: Still somewhat mechanical; doesn't capture simultaneous meaning-making.

Transactional Model

"A process of creating shared meaning through simultaneous, interdependent interaction within context."

Strength: Recognises complexity, context, and co-construction of meaning.

Critical Question

Which model best describes communication in a busy NHS ward? What are the implications of using a transmission model when the transactional model may be more accurate?

What Makes Communication "Therapeutic"?

Therapeutic Communication: A purposeful, goal-directed form of professional communication designed to facilitate healing, promote wellbeing, and support positive change in health-related behaviours and outcomes.

Key Characteristics

  • Intentional: Purposeful, not accidental
  • Patient-focused: Prioritises patient needs over practitioner convenience
  • Evidence-informed: Based on research about effective helping
  • Ethically grounded: Respects autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence
  • Contextually appropriate: Adapts to situation, culture, individual

Distinguishing Features

Social Communication

Reciprocal self-disclosure

Mutual benefit

Informal boundaries

Therapeutic Communication

Limited practitioner disclosure

Patient benefit primary

Professional boundaries

Evidence: Why Communication Matters

Research consistently demonstrates that communication quality directly impacts patient outcomes across multiple domains.

Patient Satisfaction

Communication is the strongest predictor of patient satisfaction, exceeding clinical competence and wait times.

Zolnierek & DiMatteo (2009), meta-analysis of 106 studies

Treatment Adherence

Patients whose physicians communicate effectively have 19% higher adherence rates.

Zolnierek & DiMatteo (2009)

Health Outcomes

Effective communication associated with better diabetes control, blood pressure management, and pain reduction.

Street et al. (2009)

UK Context: NHS Complaints data shows communication failures are the most common category of complaint, accounting for 35% of all formal complaints in Scotland (SPSO, 2023).

Critical Note

Correlation ≠ causation. Many studies are observational. What confounding variables might explain these associations?

Patient-Centred Communication: A Framework

Epstein & Street (2007) proposed an influential framework defining patient-centred communication through six core functions:

1
Fostering the relationship – Building trust and rapport
2
Gathering information – Understanding the patient's perspective
3
Providing information – Clear, accessible explanation
4
Making decisions – Shared decision-making
5
Enabling self-management – Supporting patient autonomy
6
Managing uncertainty – Acknowledging limits of knowledge

Scottish Healthcare Context

Healthcare Improvement Scotland's Person-Centred Care standards (2017) align with this framework, emphasising:

  • Mutually beneficial partnerships
  • Respect for individual values
  • Physical and emotional comfort
  • Information sharing

Critique

The framework assumes patients want involvement in decisions. Research suggests preferences vary by culture, age, illness severity, and individual disposition. Is "patient-centred" always patient-preferred?

The Time Myth: Quality Over Quantity

"The average GP consultation in the UK is 9.2 minutes – among the shortest in Europe." Hobbs et al. (2016), BMJ

A common concern: "I don't have time for patient-centred communication."

Research response: Effective communication techniques (e.g., agenda-setting, open questions) take seconds but significantly improve consultation quality and patient outcomes.

Mauksch et al. (2008)

Evidence Against the "Time Barrier"

  • Patient-centred consultations are not significantly longer (Levinson et al., 2000)
  • Poor communication leads to repeat visits, complaints, and errors – costing more time
  • 60-second skills (e.g., summarising, checking understanding) have disproportionate impact

Critical Question

If time isn't the primary barrier to patient-centred communication, what is? Consider: training, attitudes, systemic pressures, emotional labour...

The Role of Communication

Theoretical Foundations & Critical Analysis

Interpersonal Communication: Theoretical Foundation

Interpersonal Communication: "A distinctive, transactional form of human communication involving mutual influence, usually for the purpose of managing relationships." (Beebe, Beebe & Redmond, 2020, p. 4)

Seven Key Characteristics

Beebe, Beebe & Redmond identify interpersonal communication as:

  1. A process – ongoing, dynamic, not static events
  2. A behaviour – actions that can be observed and modified
  3. Contextual – shaped by physical, social, cultural environment
  4. Involving social cognition – thinking about self and others
  5. Intentional (usually) – purposeful, though unintentional messages occur
  6. Creating meaning – constructing understanding together
  7. Influenced by individual differences – no two interactions identical

Critical Evaluation

This framework emerged from Western communication research. How might these characteristics manifest differently in:

  • High-context cultures (e.g., indirect communication norms)?
  • Healthcare encounters with power differentials?
  • Digital/asynchronous communication?

Contemporary update: Beebe et al.'s 8th edition (2020) now addresses digital communication, but some scholars argue the framework remains rooted in face-to-face assumptions.

Communication as Process, Behaviour & Context

1. Process

Communication is continuous and cumulative. What happens in one interaction influences the next.

Healthcare implication: A patient's previous negative healthcare experience shapes how they interpret current interactions. You inherit the cumulative effect of their healthcare history.

2. Behaviour

Communication consists of observable actions – words, tone, gestures, silence. These can be learned, practised, and improved.

Healthcare implication: Communication skills are teachable. Deliberate practice with feedback improves performance (Kurtz et al., 2005).

3. Context

Communication occurs within layered contexts: physical, relational, cultural, historical, emotional.

Healthcare implication: The same words said in a private room versus a busy corridor create different meanings. Context shapes interpretation.

Social Cognition, Intentionality & Meaning

Social Cognition

The mental processes we use to perceive, remember, and interpret information about ourselves and others.

In Healthcare:

  • How patients interpret practitioner behaviour
  • How practitioners form impressions of patients
  • Mental shortcuts (heuristics) that speed up but may distort perception

Intentionality

Most communication is purposeful – we have goals we're trying to achieve. However:

  • Unintentional messages are still received
  • Impact matters more than intent ("I didn't mean to upset you" doesn't undo harm)

Meaning-Making

Meaning is not transmitted; it is co-constructed. The receiver's interpretation may differ from sender's intent.

Example: Practitioner says: "We need to talk about your weight."

Possible patient interpretations:

  • "They care about my health" (supportive)
  • "They're judging me" (threatening)
  • "Here we go again" (dismissive, based on past experience)

Critical Insight

If meaning is co-constructed, then communicating effectively requires understanding how your message might be received – not just what you intended to say.

Interpersonal Perception: How We "See" Others

Perception is the process by which we become aware of objects, events, and especially other people through our senses. It is active, subjective, and prone to error.

1. SELECTING
→
2. ORGANISING
→
3. INTERPRETING

Selecting

We cannot attend to everything. We selectively attend to certain stimuli based on:

  • Salience (what stands out)
  • Expectations (what we anticipate)
  • Needs/goals (what we're looking for)

Risk: Confirmation bias – noticing only what confirms existing beliefs.

Organising

We impose structure on incoming information using mental frameworks (schemas, categories, prototypes).

  • Grouping similar information
  • Filling in gaps with assumptions
  • Using stereotypes as cognitive shortcuts

Risk: Over-reliance on categories obscures individual uniqueness.

Interpreting

We assign meaning to what we observe, influenced by:

  • Past experience
  • Cultural background
  • Current emotional state
  • Relationship context

Risk: Fundamental attribution error – attributing behaviour to personality rather than situation.

Cognitive Biases in Impression Formation

When forming impressions of others, we are subject to systematic cognitive biases that can distort our perceptions.

Bias Description Healthcare Example
Primacy Effect First impressions disproportionately influence overall perception Patient arriving late shapes perception of "non-compliance"
Recency Effect Most recent information dominates Last interaction shapes next encounter expectations
Halo Effect One positive trait colours perception of other traits Articulate patient assumed to be more intelligent
Horn Effect One negative trait colours perception of other traits Patient with poor hygiene assumed to be non-adherent
Stereotyping Applying group characteristics to individuals Assumptions based on age, ethnicity, diagnosis

Clinical Significance

Research shows these biases contribute to diagnostic errors and healthcare disparities. Awareness is necessary but insufficient – structured approaches (e.g., checklists, diverse teams) help mitigate bias.

Barriers to Accurate Interpersonal Perception

1. Ignoring Information

Overlooking cues that don't fit our existing framework.

Example: Dismissing pain reports from patients with "drug-seeking" label.

2. Imposing Consistency

Assuming people behave consistently across situations.

Example: Expecting a "difficult" patient to be difficult in every interaction.

3. Focusing on Negatives

Negative information carries more weight than positive.

Example: One instance of missed appointments defines the patient.

4. Blaming Others

Attributing others' behaviour to character while excusing our own as situational (fundamental attribution error).

Example: "She's lazy" vs. "I was tired."

5. Avoiding Responsibility

Self-serving bias: taking credit for success, blaming external factors for failure.

Example: "My communication was fine; they just weren't listening."

Mitigation strategies: Seek disconfirming evidence, consider alternative explanations, use reflective practice, invite feedback from colleagues and patients.

Communication Barriers in Healthcare

Environmental Barriers

  • Noise, interruptions, lack of privacy
  • Time pressures, competing demands
  • Physical layout (desks as barriers)
  • Technology issues (EHR as distraction)

Patient-Related Barriers

  • Health literacy limitations
  • Language differences
  • Hearing/vision impairment
  • Cognitive impairment
  • Emotional state (anxiety, fear, grief)
  • Cultural communication norms

Practitioner-Related Barriers

  • Use of jargon and medical terminology
  • Unconscious biases (see previous slides)
  • Stress, fatigue, burnout
  • Lack of training in communication skills
  • Power differential awareness

Systemic Barriers

  • Staffing levels and skill mix
  • Organisational culture
  • Interpreter service availability
  • Continuity of care (or lack thereof)

Critical Question

Which barriers are within individual practitioner control, and which require systemic change? How does this distinction affect accountability and improvement strategies?

Case Analysis: Communication Breakdown

Scenario: Mrs. Patel, 72, was admitted to an oncology ward following surgery. During the post-operative ward round, the consultant enters, briefly glances at her notes while standing, mentions "positive margins" and "adjuvant therapy options," then asks if she has questions. Mrs. Patel shakes her head. Later, she tells her daughter she "doesn't know what's happening."

Analysis Questions (15 minutes)

  1. Using the perception process model (selecting, organising, interpreting), analyse how Mrs. Patel may have processed this interaction.
  2. Identify specific barriers to effective communication at environmental, patient, and practitioner levels.
  3. Which cognitive biases might the consultant have demonstrated? What impact might these have?
  4. Propose evidence-based interventions at individual and systemic levels to prevent similar breakdowns.

Discussion Points

  • How might Mrs. Patel's age, culture, and emotional state affect her information processing?
  • What does "shaking her head" actually tell us? Consider multiple interpretations.
  • How does the power differential in this context shape communication?
  • What is the consultant's responsibility vs. systemic responsibility?

Self-Awareness: Foundation of Therapeutic Communication

Self-Awareness: The capacity to recognise and understand one's own emotions, thoughts, values, biases, strengths, and limitations – and how these affect behaviour and interactions with others.

Why Self-Awareness Matters

  • Bias recognition: Can't mitigate what you don't notice
  • Emotional regulation: Managing your own reactions
  • Authenticity: Congruence between values and behaviour
  • Boundaries: Knowing your limits and triggers
  • Professional development: Identifying growth areas

Research evidence: Higher self-awareness in healthcare professionals is associated with:

  • Better patient outcomes (Krasner et al., 2009)
  • Reduced burnout (Shanafelt et al., 2012)
  • Fewer errors (West et al., 2006)
  • Improved teamwork (Weng et al., 2011)

Critical Consideration

Self-awareness is often discussed as an individual responsibility. But can organisations create cultures that support or undermine self-awareness? What systemic factors matter?

The Johari Window: A Framework for Self-Awareness

Developed by Joseph Luft and Harrington Ingham (1955), the Johari Window is a tool for understanding self-awareness and interpersonal relationships.

Known to Self
Unknown to Self
Known to Others

OPEN

Public self: shared knowledge

BLIND

Blind spots: others see what you don't

Unknown to Others

HIDDEN

Private self: intentionally concealed

UNKNOWN

Undiscovered: potential not yet realised

Expanding the Open Area

Therapeutic communication is enhanced when the "Open" quadrant expands. This happens through:

  • Self-disclosure: Moving from Hidden → Open (appropriately)
  • Feedback seeking: Moving from Blind → Open
  • Discovery: Moving from Unknown → Open through experience

Critique of the Model

  • Assumes feedback is available and accurate
  • Cultural assumptions about self-disclosure norms
  • Static categories vs. dynamic reality
  • Power dynamics affect what can be "opened"

Despite limitations, remains useful as a reflective tool.

Self-Awareness Reflection

Individual Reflection (10 minutes)

Consider your own communication style and tendencies. Using the Johari Window framework:

  1. OPEN: What do you know about your communication style that others also recognise? (e.g., "I'm told I'm a good listener")
  2. HIDDEN: What aspects of yourself do you deliberately manage or conceal in professional contexts? Why?
  3. BLIND: Think of feedback you've received that surprised you. What might still be in your blind spot?
  4. UNKNOWN: What communication situations do you avoid? What might you discover by engaging with them?

Pair Discussion (5 minutes)

With a partner, discuss:

  • One communication strength you're confident about
  • One area you'd like feedback on
  • How you might seek feedback more systematically in practice

Critical Extension

Consider: How might professional hierarchies, time pressures, and organisational culture affect practitioners' ability to engage in the self-disclosure and feedback-seeking the Johari model assumes?

Communication as Therapeutic Intervention

Communication is not merely a medium for delivering care – it is itself a form of care. Specific communication strategies function as evidence-based interventions.

Communication Interventions

Intervention Mechanism
Active listening Validates experience, builds trust
Empathic responses Reduces emotional distress
Information-giving Reduces anxiety, enables decisions
Teach-back method Confirms understanding, improves adherence
Motivational interviewing Resolves ambivalence, supports change

Evidence example: A systematic review found that structured communication interventions in cancer care significantly reduced patient anxiety and depression (Rodin et al., 2020).

Problem-Solving Through Communication

Effective therapeutic communication follows a problem-solving structure, moving from understanding to action.

1. EXPLORE
What's the situation? What matters to the patient?
↓
2. UNDERSTAND
What are the underlying needs, values, goals?
↓
3. ACT
What options exist? What will be done?

This structure mirrors Egan's Skilled Helper model, which you'll explore in depth in Unit 4.

Communication Skills at Each Stage

Explore: Open questions, active listening, reflection, silence

Understand: Summarising, clarifying, empathic responding, identifying themes

Act: Information-giving, shared decision-making, goal-setting, contracting

Critical Application

Problem-solving approaches assume patients have identifiable "problems" with "solutions." How might this framework need adapting for:

  • Chronic illness management?
  • End-of-life care?
  • Patients who don't see a "problem"?

Contemporary Context: Communication in Scottish Healthcare

NHS Scotland Priorities

Person-Centred Health and Care Strategy: "People will be at the centre of how health and care services are planned, delivered and improved." (Scottish Government, 2023)

Healthcare Improvement Scotland Standards

  • People are treated with dignity, respect, and compassion
  • Communication is clear, accessible, and responsive
  • People are supported to understand and manage their health

Key statistic: 67% of Scottish adults feel they are involved as much as they want to be in decisions about their care (Scottish Health Survey, 2022).

This means 33% do not – significant room for improvement.

Workforce Pressures
