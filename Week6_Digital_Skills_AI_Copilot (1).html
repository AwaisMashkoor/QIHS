Digital Transformation

Technology in Health & Social Care

Scotland's health and social care system is undergoing a digital transformation. As future practitioners, you need to understand these changes.

📱 Patient-Facing

  • NHS App — records & appointments
  • Telehealth & video consultations
  • Wearable health monitors
  • Online self-management tools

🏥 Service Delivery

  • Electronic health records
  • Digital social care records
  • AI-assisted diagnosis
  • Predictive analytics

🏠 Community & Home

  • Technology Enabled Care (TEC)
  • Assistive technology
  • Remote monitoring
  • Smart home adaptations

Why This Matters for You

Every role in health and social care now involves some level of digital skills. The SSSC, NMC and HCPC all expect practitioners to be digitally competent and to consider digital inclusion.

10 mins. Keep this conversational — ask "has anyone used the NHS App?" or "has anyone had a video GP appointment?" Make it real for them. Mention Scotland's Digital Health and Care Strategy.

Critical Thinking

The Digital Divide in Care

Not everyone benefits equally from digital health. As care professionals, you'll need to advocate for people who face digital barriers.

  • Age: Older adults may have less confidence with technology
  • Poverty: Not everyone can afford devices or internet access
  • Disability: Some digital tools aren't accessible
  • Language: Many digital services are English-only
  • Rural areas: Poor broadband connectivity
  • Digital literacy: Not everyone has the skills to navigate online services

"Digital inclusion is a social justice issue. When we move services online, we need to ask: who gets left behind?"

💭 Quick Reflection

Think about someone you know or have worked with. How might digital health services affect them positively or negatively?

Take 1 minute to think, then share with the person next to you.

5 mins. Quick think-pair-share. Connects back to Week 2's health inequalities content. Ask 1-2 students to share. Then bridge: "This is why we need to be thoughtful about how technology is used — including AI."

Understanding AI

What is Artificial Intelligence?

AI is when computer systems perform tasks that normally require human intelligence — like recognising patterns, making predictions, or generating text.

Traditional AI

Follows specific rules to perform tasks:

  • Analysing medical images (spotting tumours)
  • Predicting which patients are at risk
  • Scheduling and resource planning

Generative AI

Creates new content from what it has learned:

  • Writing text (ChatGPT, Copilot)
  • Creating images and videos
  • Answering questions conversationally

🏥 AI in Care Settings — Now

  • Diagnostic support — analysing scans & test results
  • Drug discovery and development
  • Administrative tasks — notes, scheduling
  • Chatbots for triage and information
  • Personalised treatment recommendations
  • Care documentation support

⚠️ What AI Can't Do

  • Truly understand your feelings
  • Replace human compassion & judgement
  • Know your individual circumstances
  • Be held professionally accountable
5 mins. Keep it accessible — many students won't know the difference between AI types. Ask: "How many of you have used ChatGPT or Copilot?" Get a show of hands.

Ethical Considerations

AI Ethics in Health & Social Care

AI raises important ethical questions — especially in care settings where decisions affect people's lives and wellbeing.

⚖️ Bias & Fairness

AI systems can reflect and amplify existing prejudices in society. If trained on biased data, AI can make unfair decisions about who gets care and how.

🔒 Privacy & Data

AI needs data to work. In care settings, this means sensitive personal information. Who has access? How is it protected? What happens if it's breached?

🤝 Accountability

When AI makes a mistake in a care setting, who is responsible? The developer? The organisation? The practitioner who relied on it?

🔍 Transparency

Can AI decisions be explained? Service users have a right to understand why decisions are made about their care. Many AI systems are "black boxes."

👤 Human Oversight

AI should support professional judgement, not replace it. Practitioners must maintain the skills to make independent decisions.

5 mins. Don't labour each point — the goal is awareness. Ask: "Which of these concerns you most?" Brief discussion. Bridge to: "So how do we use AI tools responsibly as students?"

What is Microsoft Copilot?

Microsoft Copilot is an AI assistant built into your Microsoft 365 account. When you sign in with your UWS credentials, you get commercial data protection — meaning your conversations aren't used to train the AI.

✅ Why Copilot (not ChatGPT)?

Responsible AI Use

The Traffic Light Framework

Before we start using Copilot, let's be clear about what's okay, what needs caution, and what's off-limits.

✓

Green — Go Ahead

Ethical, encouraged uses

  • Brainstorming — generating ideas for projects or topics
  • Explaining concepts — "explain person-centred care simply"
  • Grammar & writing style — improving your own draft
  • Study planning — creating revision timetables
  • Practising structure — "show me how a reflection is structured"
  • Translation support — understanding terms in English
!

Amber — Use with Caution

Check, verify, and be transparent

  • Research starting points — always verify with academic sources
  • Understanding terminology — but cross-check definitions
  • Feedback on drafts — but the writing must be yours
  • Reference formats — Copilot often invents fake references
  • Summarising readings — to aid understanding, not replace reading
✗

Red — Don't Do This

Academic integrity breach

  • Submitting AI text as your work — this is academic misconduct
  • Writing your reflections — only YOU can reflect on YOUR experience
  • Trusting clinical info — never rely on AI for care decisions
  • Pasting personal data — never enter patient/service user information
  • Using without checking — AI is often wrong
5 mins. Go through each column. Emphasise: "The green column is what we're going to practise today." Ask: "Can anyone think of something that might be hard to categorise?" Good discussion point.

Academic Integrity

What UWS Says About AI

UWS doesn't ban AI — but it does require you to use it honestly and appropriately.

Submitting "material generated by artificial intelligence where such material has been specifically deemed unauthorised for that assessment item" is a breach of academic integrity.

— UWS Student Academic Integrity Procedure, 2023

In plain English: always check your module handbook to see if and how AI can be used for each specific assessment.

UWS Library's 5-Point Check

Before using AI for assessed work, ask yourself:

1

Have I checked if AI use is permitted for this assessment?

2

Did I follow the specific guidance on how AI can be used?

3

Can I honestly say I am the author of this work?

4

Did I consult multiple sources, not just AI?

5

Have I credited AI tools I used? (UWS Library has referencing guidance)

3 mins. Be clear but not scary. Emphasise: "For ASPIRE, your reflections must be YOUR words about YOUR experience. AI can't reflect for you. But you can use Copilot to help you understand concepts, plan your studies, and improve your writing."

Traffic Light Scenarios — Answers

Let's see how you did. Some of these are genuinely debatable — and that's the point.

🟢 GREEN

Scenario A

Using Copilot to understand Gibbs' model, then writing your own reflection. This is a learning tool — like asking a tutor to explain something. The thinking and writing is still yours.

🟡 AMBER

Scenario B

Fixing grammar is okay — but "make it more academic-sounding" risks changing your voice. Reflections should sound like you, not like AI. Stick to grammar corrections only.

🔴 RED

Scenario C

This replaces learning entirely. The AI summary might be inaccurate, and you haven't engaged with the material. You can't reflect on learning that didn't happen.

🟡 AMBER

Scenario D

A useful starting point — but you must verify every statistic from an official source (e.g. Public Health Scotland, Scottish Government). AI frequently invents plausible-sounding data.

🔴 RED

Scenario E

Never use AI for clinical decisions. AI can hallucinate medical information. On placement, ask your supervisor or check approved clinical resources. Patient safety comes first.

🔴 RED

Scenario F

If AI rewrites your entire Action Plan, it's no longer your work. This is academic misconduct. Getting grammar feedback is fine — handing over authorship is not.

The Grey Areas Are the Point

Real life doesn't come with clear labels. The skill you're building here is ethical judgement — the same skill you'll need as a care professional making decisions every day. When in doubt, ask your lecturer.

5 mins. Go through each answer. Scenario B sparks the best debate — validate that it IS genuinely tricky. The line between "fix grammar" and "rewrite" is blurry, which is why amber exists. Key message: when in doubt, ask.

Practical Skills

Writing Better Prompts

The quality of what you get from Copilot depends on the quality of what you ask. Here are some tips.

✅ Good Prompt Ingredients

  • Context: Tell it who you are — "I'm a first-year health and social care student"
  • Specific task: Be clear about what you want — "explain", "compare", "suggest"
  • Constraints: Set boundaries — "in simple terms", "in 3 bullet points"
  • Purpose: Say why — "I'm trying to understand this for my essay"

❌ Common Mistakes

  • Too vague: "Tell me about care" → what kind? where? for whom?
  • Asking it to write FOR you: "Write my essay on..." → academic misconduct
  • Not checking output: Taking the first response as truth
  • Sharing personal info: Never enter real names or patient data
Example: Before and After
❌ "What is sustainability?"
✅ "I'm a first-year health and social care student in Scotland. Can you explain how environmental sustainability affects health outcomes, with one example from an NHS Scotland context? Keep it to about 200 words."
The second prompt gives context, specificity, a relevant setting, and a length constraint — producing a much more useful response.
5 mins. Show the before/after live in Copilot — the difference is striking. Encourage students to refine prompts iteratively: "If the first answer isn't right, tell Copilot what to change."

Connecting Themes

Sustainability & Health: A Quick Connection

Sustainability isn't just about the environment — it's about building health and care systems that can last and serve everyone fairly.

🌍 Environmental

  • NHS Scotland aims for net zero by 2040
  • Climate change increases health risks
  • Air pollution, flooding, heat — all affect vulnerable people most

👥 Social

  • Sustainable services mean fair access for all
  • Workforce wellbeing — preventing burnout
  • Community-based care reduces resource use

💰 Economic

  • Prevention is cheaper than treatment
  • Digital tools can reduce waste and cost
  • Sustainable funding models for social care

💭 Connection to Your Group Project

Whatever your group topic, there's a sustainability angle. How does your topic connect to building care systems that are fair, lasting, and environmentally responsible? This could strengthen your group project and your reflections.
