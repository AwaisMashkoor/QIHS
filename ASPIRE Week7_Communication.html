Part 1

Communicating
in Teams

What makes it work — and what gets in the way

Why It's Different

Team Communication ≠ One-to-One

In a team, you're not just sharing information — you're navigating roles, styles, and social dynamics.

🛡️ Psychological Safety

The best teams aren't the ones that make no mistakes — they're the ones where people feel safe to speak up, ask questions, and admit when they don't know.

🎯 Shared Understanding

Teams break down when people assume everyone is on the same page. Good team communication means checking, not assuming.

⚖️ Different Styles

Some people think out loud. Others need time to process. Neither is wrong — but teams need to make room for both.

Key question: Do you feel you can say what you actually think in your group? If yes — that's psychological safety working. If not — what would need to change?
Practical Habits

Habits That Help Teams Communicate

Small, practical things that make a real difference.

✅ Check In, Don't Check Up

"How are you getting on with your section?" shows genuine interest.
"Have you done your section yet?" creates pressure.

The difference is tone, not words.

💡 Disagree with the Idea, Not the Person

"I see it differently — what if we tried…" is constructive.
"That's wrong" shuts the conversation down.

In care teams, this skill protects patients.

🗣️ Share the Airtime

Research shows groups with equal turn-taking outperform groups dominated by one voice — regardless of individual ability.

Invite quieter voices in. Make space.

🐘 Name the Elephant

If something isn't working — say so. "I think we're stuck" or "I'm not sure we all agree" is better than pretending.

Avoidance rarely makes things better.

Key Skill

Navigating Disagreement

Disagreement isn't the problem. It's how you handle it that matters.

😶 Passive

Staying quiet to avoid conflict. Going along with things you don't agree with. Not sharing your view.

Risk: Resentment builds. Issues stay unresolved. You don't learn.

💪 Assertive

Expressing your view clearly and respectfully. Listening to others. Looking for solutions together.

Result: Better decisions. Mutual respect. Stronger teams.

😤 Aggressive

Dominating the conversation. Dismissing others' views. Insisting on being right.

Risk: Trust breaks down. People withdraw. The team fragments.

Assertive sentence starters: "I see it differently because…" · "I'm not sure I agree — can we talk it through?" · "I think there's another way to look at this" · "Can I share a concern?"

Part 2

Giving & Receiving
Feedback

The team communication skill that accelerates growth

Why It Matters

The Power of Feedback

Feedback is one of the most powerful influences on learning and achievement — but it must be done well.
— John Hattie, Visible Learning
Remember Week 4? A growth mindset treats feedback as information, not judgement. That shift changes everything.

In Your Career, Feedback Will Be…

  • How supervisors help you develop
  • How colleagues keep each other safe
  • How teams improve together
  • How service users tell you what matters
  • Sometimes uncomfortable — and that's OK
Two-way: Sometimes you'll need to give feedback to people more senior than you. That takes skill — and you're building it now.
Framework

Giving Constructive Feedback

Good feedback is specific, kind, and focused on actions — not personality.

Key Principles

1
Be specific — focus on actions, not personality
2
Be timely — give feedback close to the event
3
Be balanced — strengths and development areas
4
Use "I" language — "I noticed…" not "You always…"
5
Offer a path forward — suggestions, not just critique

Instead of… → Try…

❌ "This isn't great"
✅ "The intro is clear — the middle section needs more evidence"
❌ "You need to do more"
✅ "I'd value your input on X — your perspective would strengthen it"
❌ "Great job!"
✅ "The NHS data in your research section really strengthens the argument"
Structured Model

Pendleton's Rules

A structured feedback model used in healthcare education — and what you'll use today.

1
Learner speaks first: What did I / we do well? What am I pleased with?
2
Observer adds: What else went well? What strengths did I notice?
3
Learner reflects: What would I do differently? Where could I improve?
4
Observer adds: What else could develop? Any suggestions?
Why learner-first? It gives you agency over your own development. People are often more honest about their own areas for growth than any observer — and it builds the self-awareness that reflective practice depends on.
The Other Side

Receiving Feedback Well

Often harder than giving it — but just as important.

Common Reactions

It's normal to feel defensive. Three common triggers:

🎯 Truth Triggers

"That's not right — they've misunderstood"

🤝 Relationship Triggers

"Who are they to tell me this?"

🪞 Identity Triggers

"Does this mean I'm not good enough?"

What To Do

1
Listen fully — resist preparing your defence
2
Say thank you — it's a complete sentence. It buys you time.
3
Ask questions — "Can you give me an example?"
4
Take time — you don't have to respond immediately
5
Decide what to keep — not all feedback needs action
Assertive receiving: neither collapsing into agreement nor becoming defensive.

Main Activity — Setup

Peer Feedback on Group Projects

Putting today's skills into practice. Using Pendleton's Rules.

1
Pair up with another group — you'll give feedback to each other
2
Group A presents (3 mins) — What's your topic? Where are you? Any challenges?
3
Pendleton's feedback (5 mins) — Group A self-assesses first, then Group B gives feedback
4
Switch — Group B presents, Group A gives feedback
5
Note it down — What feedback did you get? What will you act on? (Use for your reflection)
Remember: This is practice in a professional skill. Give feedback the way you'd want to receive it — specific, kind, and useful.
Activity — 20 minutes

Peer Feedback: Go!

3 min present → 5 min Pendleton's → switch → repeat

While Giving Feedback…

  • Be specific — what exactly works?
  • Be constructive — how could it improve?
  • Use "I" language
  • Offer suggestions, not instructions

While Receiving Feedback…

  • Listen without defending
  • Write down what you hear
  • Ask clarifying questions
  • Say thank you — then decide what to use
